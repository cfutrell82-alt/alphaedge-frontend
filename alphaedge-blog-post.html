The Complete Guide to Reading Trade Signals: Entry, Target, and Stop Loss Explained

JM
James M.
June 20, 2026
8 min read

If you're new to AlphaEdge, the first thing you'll notice is that every signal we send has several data points: an asset name, a direction (BUY or SELL), an entry price, a target, and a stop loss. Understanding what each of these means — and how to act on them — is the foundation of trading with our signals.

This guide breaks it all down so you can go from receiving your first signal to placing your first trade with confidence.

What Does a Signal Actually Look Like?

Here's an example of a real AlphaEdge signal:

📡 AlphaEdge Signal — Jun 20, 2026
BUY
AAPL — Momentum Breakout
Entry
$189.40
Target (TP)
$197.00
Stop Loss (SL)
$185.00

Breaking Down Each Component

1. The Direction: BUY or SELL

BUY means we expect the price to go up. You enter a long position and profit if the price rises to your target. SELL (or SHORT) means we expect the price to fall. You enter a short position and profit if the price drops to your target.

💡 Note: For spot crypto and stock traders without short-selling access, only act on BUY signals unless you have a platform that supports shorting.

2. The Entry Price

The entry price is the price at which we recommend entering the trade. In the example above, that's $189.40 for AAPL. Ideally you want to enter as close to this price as possible — within about 0.5% is typically acceptable.

If the price has already moved significantly past the entry by the time you see the signal, it's usually better to skip it and wait for the next one rather than chasing.

3. The Take Profit (Target)

The take profit — or TP — is your exit price when the trade goes in your favor. In our example, that's $197.00. When AAPL hits $197.00, you close the trade and lock in your profit.

✅ Pro tip: Some traders take partial profits at the target (e.g. close 50% of the position) and let the rest run with a trailing stop. This is a valid strategy but for beginners, simply closing the full position at the target is cleanest.

4. The Stop Loss

The stop loss — or SL — is your exit price if the trade goes against you. In our example, that's $185.00. If AAPL drops to $185.00, you close the trade to limit your loss.

The stop loss is not optional. It is the single most important part of the signal. Every trade has risk, and the stop loss is how you manage it.

⚠️ Warning: Never remove your stop loss hoping the price will come back. This is how traders blow accounts. Respect the SL every time, no exceptions.

Understanding Risk/Reward

The risk/reward ratio (R/R) tells you how much you stand to gain versus how much you could lose on a trade. In our AAPL example:

  • Risk: $189.40 − $185.00 = $4.40 per share
  • Reward: $197.00 − $189.40 = $7.60 per share
  • R/R ratio: $7.60 / $4.40 = 1:1.7

AlphaEdge targets a minimum R/R of 1:2 on every signal. This means even if you only win 50% of your trades, you still come out ahead mathematically.

Position Sizing: How Much Should You Trade?

Never risk more than 1–2% of your total account on a single trade. This is the rule that keeps you in the game long enough to win.

Here's the formula:

Position size = (Account size × Risk %) ÷ (Entry − Stop Loss)

Example: $10,000 account, 1% risk, entry $189.40, SL $185.00:

$10,000 × 0.01 = $100 ÷ $4.40 = 22 shares

What to Do When You Receive a Signal

  1. Check the entry price — is the current price close to the entry?
  2. Calculate your position size based on your account and risk tolerance
  3. Place your order with a stop loss set at the SL price
  4. Set a take profit order at the TP price
  5. Walk away and let the trade play out

Final Thoughts

Reading signals is simple once you understand the components. The harder part — and the part that separates profitable traders from losing ones — is the discipline to follow the plan: enter at the right price, set your stop loss, and stick to it.
